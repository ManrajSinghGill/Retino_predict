What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is a complication of diabetes that affects the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. High blood sugar levels damage the retinal blood vessels, leading to vision problems or blindness if untreated.

Symptoms

  • Blurred or fluctuating vision
  • Dark spots or floaters
  • Loss of central or peripheral vision
  • Difficulty seeing at night

Stages of Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic Retinopathy progresses through the following stages:

  1. Mild Nonproliferative Retinopathy: Early-stage damage with small retinal swelling.
  2. Moderate Nonproliferative Retinopathy: Blockage of blood vessels limits retinal blood supply.
  3. Severe Nonproliferative Retinopathy: More extensive blockages and new vessel growth signals.
  4. Proliferative Retinopathy: Advanced stage with abnormal blood vessel growth causing bleeding or detachment.

Knowledge Center

Prevention

  • Maintain optimal blood sugar levels.
  • Control blood pressure and cholesterol.
  • Undergo regular eye exams—at least once a year.
  • Adopt a healthy diet and exercise regularly.

Treatment Options

  • Laser Surgery: Seals leaking blood vessels.
  • Anti-VEGF Injections: Reduces abnormal vessel growth.
  • Vitrectomy: Removes blood or scar tissue from the eye.
  • Blood Sugar Management: Supports recovery.
